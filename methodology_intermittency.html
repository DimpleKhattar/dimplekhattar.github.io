Module 03 . Intermittency

West Texas Negative Pricing & Renewable Intermittency

How we analysed ERCOT generation data to quantify the frequency, depth, and hourly pattern of negative prices at the HB-WEST hub, and how this connects to both the BESS strategy and the outage risk at the West data center.

What is it?

Negative electricity pricing in ERCOT

In a deregulated electricity market, prices can go negative when supply exceeds demand and generators cannot be economically curtailed quickly. West Texas is ERCOT's most renewable-intensive zone: wind farms across the Permian Basin and solar farms throughout the region can collectively generate more power than the local grid can absorb.

Three mechanisms drive negative prices at HB-WEST:

Wind curtailment

Wind turbines generate when wind blows regardless of demand. When wind output exceeds transmission capacity east to Houston and Dallas, local prices go negative to incentivize consumption.

Solar curtailment

Solar peaks 10:00–16:00. In spring, solar output routinely exceeds local load. 50.7% of all negative price hours at West occur in this 6-hour window.

Transmission congestion

West Texas generation cannot always flow east due to transmission limits. Locally stranded power means local prices go negative even when the rest of ERCOT has normal prices.

A negative price means generators are paying consumers to take electricity. From the data center operator's perspective: when training, every negative-price hour is a payment received, not a cost incurred.

How does it fit here?

Three downstream uses of this analysis

The intermittency analysis feeds three other modules:

BESS valuation

Negative price hours are the BESS charging window. The depth and frequency of negative prices determines the TB1/TB2 daily spread, the core input to battery P&L.

LSMC training schedule

The 10:00–16:00 solar trough window identified here becomes the LSMC exercise region at West. The optimal boundary θ* = −$0.42/MWh is calibrated from this hourly pattern.

NBB-SO linkage

wind_speed is the dominant feature in both F_μ and F_α at West. High-wind hours = cheapest power AND highest outage risk. The BESS cannot do both simultaneously.

Implications

The central strategic tension at West

The cheapest hours are the riskiest hours. The same high-wind, high-solar conditions that push LMP negative also drive the highest outage probability at West (NBB-SO finding). An operator scheduling all training in the 10:00–16:00 window is simultaneously maximising cost savings and maximising outage exposure.

49.4% solar trough hours are negative. For a data center running 24/7 inference workloads that can shift GPU training to these windows, this represents a massive cost opportunity. Scheduling training exclusively in negative-price hours produces a negative average daily power cost.

The virtual BESS captures price spread without operational complexity. The 607 negative-price hours provide a wide daily spread (P_min often negative, P_max typically $30–$60). The TB2 spread averages $152/MWh. A virtual swap captures this without requiring a physical battery to be at the right state of charge at the right time.

Physical BESS has an operational conflict. A physical battery cannot simultaneously be: (a) fully charged for arbitrage after a negative-price window and (b) available as full backup power for a wind-driven outage. The intermittency analysis is the justification for keeping physical BESS as insurance, not arbitrage.

The core West site tension: The single variable (wind_speed) that creates the most valuable charging windows also creates the highest outage risk. Any operating policy that treats negative prices as pure opportunity, without accounting for simultaneous grid fragility, is incomplete. The LSMC boundary at −$0.42/MWh implicitly handles this by running training (not BESS charge) during these windows, decoupling the scheduling opportunity from the physical battery constraint.
