Module 04 . Options Pricing

Bachelier Normal Call & Spark Spread Strip

How we valued the Houston gas generator tolling agreement as a strip of Bachelier normal call options on the spark spread, and why the Bachelier model is required when the forward price is negative.

What is it?

The Bachelier normal model

The standard Black-Scholes model assumes the underlying asset follows a log-normal distribution, meaning it can never be negative. For a single commodity option (oil, gas), this is reasonable. But for a spread option such as the spark spread (LMP minus gas cost), the underlying can and does go negative. Black-Scholes breaks down.

The Bachelier normal model (1900) instead assumes the underlying follows a normal (Gaussian) distribution. It can handle negative forwards and strikes, making it the correct model for energy spreads.

Bachelier normal call price C = (F − K) · N(d) + σ √T · n(d)
d = (F − K) / (σ √T)
N(·) = standard normal CDF  |  n(·) = standard normal PDF
F = forward spread  |  K = strike  |  T = time to expiry  |  σ = normal vol

Our specific application uses an offset-adjusted Bachelier formulation when the forward spark spread F is negative (which it is: F = −$29.04/MWh). We shift both F and K by the same positive offset to make F positive, compute the call, and the economics are unchanged because the payoff only depends on (F − K), not the absolute levels.

How does it fit here?

Valuing the tolling agreement as a call strip

The Houston tolling agreement grants the right to dispatch the gas generator each hour at a fixed heat rate. This is equivalent to owning a strip of 4,380 hourly European call options on the spark spread, one for each hour over the six-month horizon.

What the spark spread is

Spark spread ($/MWh) = LMP − (HH + $3.00) × 9.5

The generator is exercised (dispatched) when spark spread > 0. The tolling fee ($3.00/MMBtu O&M adder) raises the effective gas cost above Henry Hub spot.

Why the forward is negative

Averaging Feb–Apr data: mean LMP_HOU = $26.84/MWh, mean gas cost = (3.14 + 3.00) × 9.5 = $58.33/MWh. Mean spark spread F = 26.84 − 58.33 = −$29.04/MWh. Generator is out-of-the-money on average, but has significant option value due to volatility spikes.

A negative forward spread does not mean the option is worthless; it means the option is deeply out-of-the-money on the mean path. With σ_spark = $48.69/MWh and a 1-month horizon, the right tail of the distribution extends well into positive territory. The Bachelier call price captures this tail value.

How did we work on it?

Derivation and computation

01
Compute the spark spread series. For each of the 2,135 hourly observations: SS_t = lmp_houston_t − (hh_t + 3.00) × 9.5. Filter to non-zero gas price hours only.
02
Estimate daily spark spread volatility. Standard deviation of the daily mean spark spread = $48.69/MWh. Annualise: σ_ann = $48.69 × √252 = $772/MWh (but we use the annualised percentage vol = 51.34%/yr decomposed from GBM components for consistency with the calibration).
03
Price each monthly option. For each calendar month (Feb, Mar, Apr, May, Jun, Jul), compute a Bachelier call with: F = month forward spark spread, K = 0 (at-the-money-forward strike), T = time to month midpoint, σ = calibrated normal vol. Multiply by total MW-hours in the month (capacity × hours).
04
Sum the strip. Monthly option values are summed to produce the total strip value. Three vol scenarios: base (48.69/MWh), low (40/MWh), high (60/MWh).

Spark spread volatility decomposition

Combined volatility formula σ_spark = √(σ_LMP² + (HR · σ_gas)² − 2 · HR · ρ · σ_LMP · σ_gas)
= √(46.3² + (9.5 × 34.5)² − 2 · 9.5 · (−0.044) · 46.3 · 34.5)
= √(2143.69 + 107,255.06 + 1,335.54)
= √110,734.29 = 332.9/yr (annualised basis points)
In spread units: effectively 51.34%/yr vol on the spread level

Note the negative correlation ρ = −0.044 adds to spread volatility: because LMP and gas tend to move in the same direction (both up or both down), the spread is more volatile than each component. The −2·HR·ρ term becomes positive, increasing σ_spark.

The option value is substantial even with a deeply negative forward because volatility is large relative to the distance from zero. σ = $48.69/MWh vs F = −$29.04/MWh: the forward is only 0.6 standard deviations out-of-the-money on a monthly basis. Options this close to the money have significant time value.

Implications

Why options pricing changes the investment decision

Naive cost analysis says don't bother. At a negative mean spark spread of −$29/MWh, an analyst using expected values would recommend not dispatching the generator in any month other than April. This analysis would value the tolling agreement at zero or negative.

Options pricing reveals $4.73M of value. The right to dispatch (not the obligation) transforms the negative-expected-value asset into a positive-valued option. The Bachelier model captures exactly this asymmetry: unlimited upside from spikes, floored downside at zero (we simply don't dispatch).

Bachelier is required, not optional. Black-Scholes would require a positive forward. Applying it naively to a negative forward (e.g., by shifting the scale) introduces pricing errors. The Bachelier model is the correct choice for spread options and is standard practice in energy markets.

The tolling fee threshold matters. At the base case, the tolling agreement is worth holding at any cost below $4.73M. At high vol, the threshold rises to $6.18M. The operator should renew or extend the agreement at any price below the low-vol estimate of $3.63M for a certainty margin.

The options pricing module is where the generator transforms from a "backup power source" into a financial instrument. The $3.63M–$6.18M strip value quantifies exactly what the operator would pay to keep the right-to-dispatch, and therefore what price the counterparty can reasonably demand for the tolling agreement.
