Module 05 . Battery Valuation

TB-x Swap Structure & BESS Economics

How we valued battery energy storage at the West Texas site using the TB-x (Time-Block spread) swap framework, compared physical vs virtual BESS economics, and determined that the virtual swap dominates while physical BESS serves only as outage insurance.

What is it?

TB-x swaps and daily price spreads

A TB-x swap (Time-Block spread swap) settles on the difference between peak and off-peak electricity prices within a day. The x denotes the number of price blocks used. Physical BESS is the hardware implementation of the same arbitrage (charge at low prices, discharge at high) but carries capital cost, efficiency losses, and a defined degradation curve.

TB1: single daily spread

Daily max minus daily min LMP. The theoretical maximum if you could charge at the exact cheapest hour and discharge at the most expensive. A performance benchmark, not achievable without perfect foresight.

TB1 = max(LMP_t) − min(LMP_t)  for t in day d

TB2: two-block spread

Sum of top-2 prices minus sum of bottom-2 prices. More realistic: captures two charge and two discharge cycles per day. This is the settlement basis for the virtual swap and most physical BESS P&L modelling.

TB2 = (P_max1 + P_max2) − (P_min1 + P_min2)
How does it fit here?

Physical vs virtual: the key comparison

Physical BESS

Own or lease a 40 MWh battery. Charge during negative-price hours, discharge during spikes. Captures spread in real power flows. Capital cost: $90M. Round-trip efficiency loss: 8%. Degradation baked into LCOS.

Unique benefit: physical backup power during grid outage; no financial instrument replicates this.

Not viable for arbitrage at current spreads
vs

Virtual TBx Swap

Financial contract: receive TB2 settlement daily, pay a fixed fee per MWh. No hardware, no efficiency loss, no degradation, zero capital outlay. Counterparty assumes the physical scheduling complexity.

Limitation: no physical power delivery; it does not protect against outage.

+$204,789 net over 6 months at West
How did we work on it?

LCOS calculation and breakeven analysis

Step 1: Levelized Cost of Storage (LCOS)

LCOS converts the upfront capital and operating cost of the physical battery into a per-MWh figure that can be compared directly to the spread it captures:

LCOS formula LCOS = Total lifetime cost / Total lifetime MWh discharged
= $90M / (40 MWh × 2 cycles/day × 0.92 efficiency × 365 days × 10 yr)
= $90M / 728,640 MWh = $123.52/MWh

Step 2: Net settlement per MWh (physical)

The 8% round-trip efficiency loss means the battery only delivers 0.92 MWh of discharge per MWh charged. Net capture per MWh:

Physical BESS net settlement Net = (TB2 × 0.92) − LCOS
At mean West TB2 = $152.05/MWh:
Net = ($152.05 × 0.92) − $123.52 = $139.89 − $123.52 = +$16.37/MWh  (marginal positive)
But full capital breakeven requires spread of $445.47/MWh, far above the historical mean

Step 3: Virtual swap 6-month P&L projection

Virtual swap P&L (West, 40 MW, 183 days) Gross = Σ_d [TB2_d × MW] = $152.05 × 40 × 183 = $1,114,206
Less fixed fee = $140.18 × 40 × 183 = $1,025,718  (max fee scenario)
Net at max fee = +$88,488  |  Net at $120/MWh fee = +$204,789

Step 4: Houston site comparison

Houston TB2 spreads are narrower than West because the price distribution is less skewed, with fewer deeply negative hours and fewer extreme spike hours. Houston physical BESS breakeven exceeds West. The West site is the better candidate for either physical or virtual BESS. Since the physical case is borderline at West, Houston physical BESS is clearly not viable.

Implications

What battery economics mean for the strategy

Do not build physical BESS for arbitrage. At $445/MWh breakeven versus $152/MWh historical mean spread, physical BESS requires a 3× improvement in daily spread to pencil out. ERCOT West data shows no sustained period where this has held. The business case does not exist at current spreads.

Enter the virtual TBx swap. At a fixed fee below $140.18/MWh, the virtual swap is positive NPV. Negotiate a fixed fee of $100–$120/MWh. This generates $204K–$318K net over 6 months with zero capital risk and zero operational overhead.

Retain existing physical BESS as outage insurance only. If a battery is already installed, keep it. Not for arbitrage, but for resilience. The NBB-SO model shows that wind_speed dominates both outage frequency and overdispersion at West. The same conditions that create negative prices (high wind) create the highest risk of grid disconnection. A charged battery provides ride-through capability during these events.

Review trigger: TB2 > $300/MWh sustained for one quarter. If renewable over-penetration in West ERCOT deepens spreads further and TB2 sustains above $300/MWh, revisit the physical arbitrage case. Set this as a quarterly monitoring threshold.

The virtual swap captures the financial value of the price spread without requiring the physical battery to be in the right state of charge at the right hour. It removes operational complexity and capital burden while preserving the economic upside. The physical battery is kept for a different purpose: actual electrons during an outage.
