Module 01 . Price Dynamics

GBM Calibration & Monte Carlo Simulation

We modelled electricity and natural gas price uncertainty using GBM, calibrated on 2,135 hourly ERCOT observations, to generate 10,000 forward price paths for options and battery valuation.

What is GBM?

Geometric Brownian Motion

GBM is the standard continuous-time model for commodity and energy price dynamics. It assumes log-prices follow a random walk with drift, producing log-normally distributed terminal prices. The stochastic differential equation is:

GBM SDE dS = μ · S · dt + σ · S · dW

where S is the price level, μ is the drift rate, σ is the volatility, dW is an increment of a Wiener process (Brownian motion), and dt is an infinitesimal time step. The solution in discrete form is:

Discrete solution S(t + Δt) = S(t) · exp{ (μ − σ²/2)Δt + σ√Δt · Z }
where Z ~ N(0,1) is a standard normal random draw

GBM is appropriate here because electricity and gas prices: (a) are always positive, (b) show mean-reverting-like behaviour in the log-return space, and (c) have well-understood volatility structures that can be estimated from historical data.

How does it fit here?

Why price uncertainty is the foundation

Every quantitative piece of the strategy depends on knowing the distribution of future prices:

Generator dispatch (IMHR)

Spark spread = LMP − Heat Rate × Gas price. The volatility of both legs, and the correlation between them, determines how often the generator is in-the-money and what the option strip is worth.

LSMC training schedule

LSMC backward induction runs on 10,000 simulated price paths. If the price paths are wrong, the exercise boundary is wrong. GBM calibration is the first module because every downstream output depends on it.

The GBM parameters σ_HOU = 46.3%/yr and σ_WEST = 62.0%/yr are the single most important calibration inputs. They determine the width of the Bachelier call distribution, the LSMC option value, and the 10,000-path fan chart confidence intervals.

How did we work on it?

MLE calibration and correlated path generation

Step 1: Maximum Likelihood Estimation of parameters

Log-returns of hourly LMP and Henry Hub are computed, then MLE is used to fit μ (drift) and σ (volatility) for each series separately:

Log-return r_t = ln(S_t / S_{t-1})
μ_MLE = μ_hat + σ²/2  (Ito correction)
σ_MLE = std(r_t) × √(annualisation factor)

Step 2: Correlation structure

The three series (HB-HOUSTON, HB-WEST, Henry Hub) are correlated. We compute the Pearson correlation matrix of log-returns and use Cholesky decomposition to generate correlated standard normal vectors for each simulation step:

Cholesky decomposition Σ = L · Lᵀ  where L is lower triangular
Z_corr = L · Z_ind  where Z_ind ~ N(0, I)

Step 3: 10,000-path Monte Carlo

Starting from the last observed price in each series, we simulate 10,000 paths forward over a 1-year horizon, stepping hourly. Each path produces a terminal price distribution used for VaR and CVaR estimation.

The negative correlation ρ(HOU, HH) = −0.044 is economically important: when gas prices spike (expensive fuel), LMPs also tend to spike, but the relationship is weak. This means the spark spread distribution is wide: gas cost increases do not reliably offset LMP increases. The generator retains option value even when gas is expensive.

Monte Carlo terminal

HB-HOUSTON 1-yr VaR(95%) = $142/MWh; CVaR(95%) = $178/MWh. West 1-yr VaR(95%) = $167/MWh reflecting higher vol.

Spark spread

σ_spark = √(σ_LMP² + (HR·σ_gas)² − 2·HR·ρ·σ_LMP·σ_gas) = 51.34%/yr. The negative correlation adds vol to the spread because gas and LMP move in the same direction infrequently.

Rolling stability

168-hour (1-week) rolling window shows σ_WEST is structurally higher than σ_HOU in all windows. Parameters are not constant, but the ranking is stable.

Implications

What the calibration means for the strategy

West is structurally more volatile. σ_WEST = 62.0%/yr vs σ_HOU = 46.3%/yr. Every options calculation using West LMP will show wider distributions, higher option premia, and larger LSMC gains. West is the higher-value site from a financial options perspective.

The near-zero LMP-gas correlation matters. ρ = −0.044 means the generator is not a perfect hedge for grid price risk. When grid LMP is high (bad for the buyer), gas costs may also be high (bad for the generator owner). The tolling agreement is valuable precisely because it caps the downside on the gas side.

Fat tails justify the real options approach. Both series reject normality (Shapiro-Wilk p < 0.01). Bachelier normal pricing is a first-order approximation, but it is more appropriate than Black-Scholes here because the forward spark spread can be negative, which Black-Scholes cannot handle directly.

LSMC requires GBM paths. The 10,000 Monte Carlo paths from this calibration feed directly into the LSMC backward induction. The exercise boundary θ* = −$0.42/MWh at West and θ* = $121/MWh at Houston peak hours are computed from these simulations.

The calibrated volatility parameters confirm that power procurement at both sites has meaningful option value. At σ = 62%/yr, even a short-dated (1-month) at-the-money option on West LMP is worth several dollars per MWh, far more than the cost of the financial instruments used to capture it.
